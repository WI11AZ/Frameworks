About the Competency Areas

The NICE Framework Competency Areas represent a revolutionary approach to cybersecurity workforce development, focusing on learner capabilities rather than just job functions.

What are Competency Areas?

Definition: Clusters of related Knowledge and Skill statements that correlate with one's capability to perform Tasks in a particular domain.

  • Focus on learner capabilities: What they know and can do
  • vs Work Roles: What work needs to be done
  • Building block approach: Using TKS (Task, Knowledge, Skill) statements
  • Domain-specific: Organized around cybersecurity specializations

Purpose and Benefits

  • Bridge skills-based hiring gaps: Focus on what candidates can actually do
  • Enable competency-based assessment: Measure capabilities, not just experience
  • Support emerging technology domains: Address new cybersecurity challenges
  • Complement traditional Work Roles: Provide additional workforce flexibility
  • Facilitate workforce mobility: Enable career transitions and development

Matrix Approach

Instead of a simple linear alignment, the NCWF structures Competency Areas (groups of related knowledge and skills) and Work Roles (cybersecurity job functions defined by tasks and KSATs) within a matrix:

  • A Competency Area may apply to multiple Work Roles: For example, the "Cyber Resiliency" competency may be relevant for OT engineering, threat analysis, or incident response.
  • A Work Role may require multiple Competency Areas: A network architect role could require competencies in network engineering, access management, and risk management, each defined by a distinct NF-COM.

The Knowledge (K) and Skills (S) statements from Competency Areas are "activated", supplemented, and sometimes shared across several Work Roles, reflecting frequent intersections and overlaps in practice.

This enables a modular and evolving approach, especially for hybrid profiles, personalized training, or adapting to new technological challenges.

Benefits of the Matrix Structure

  • Personalization of skills development and career paths.
  • Adaptability to the evolution of professions and the emergence of new cybersecurity domains.
  • Clarity for employers, who can assess needs across multiple dimensions, and for professionals, who can precisely position themselves within the ecosystem.

This matrix-based integration is thus a pillar of the robustness and agility of NCWF v2.0.0, facilitating dynamic skills management and the formation of effective teams to address the diversity of cyber threats.

Relationship to Work Roles

  • Complementary: Work together or separately with existing Work Roles
  • Additive: Can overlay onto one or more Work Roles for enhanced capabilities
  • Cross-functional: May span multiple Work Roles in different categories
  • Emerging: Represent domains not yet established as traditional Work Roles
  • Non-duplicative: Do not replace or duplicate existing Work Roles

NCWF and DCWF Integration

NCWF (National Cybersecurity Workforce Framework)

Civilian cybersecurity workforce development

DCWF (DoD Cyber Workforce Framework)

Military/defense cybersecurity workforce development

Matrix Relationship: Competency Areas provide horizontal capabilities across vertical Work Roles in both frameworks.

Flexibility: Organizations can use either framework or both depending on their specific context and needs.

Building Blocks Structure (TKS)

Task Statements (T-statements)

Describe specific work activities directed toward achievement of organizational objectives

Knowledge Statements (K-statements)

Body of information that must be applied to successfully perform functions

Skill Statements (S-statements)

Observable competencies required to perform learned activities

Assessment: Typically conducted at the Competency Area level rather than individual Task level (unlike Work Roles).
